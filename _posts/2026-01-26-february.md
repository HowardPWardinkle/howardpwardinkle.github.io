## Idea

After three consecutive months of mice, I knew I needed a break. [Study in Black and White IX](/2025/05/31/june.html) was was in June 2025, so I thought I was due for another one. Trying to come up with a new shape is getting harder and harder. This time I thought of doing the first 90&deg; b&w study. I toyed with ideas like patterns that shifted as you moved the card around. After doing some sketches, I came up with the diamond pattern.

## Design

The first sketch was a 16x8 grid, as shown on the upper left below. I've already done squares, so I thought if I skewed the squares into diamond shapes, it would be more interesting.

![sketches](/images/2602/sketches.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Five sketches with the final in the front*{: .image-caption}

Sketch #2 used the 16x8 grid at an angle, but symmetrical with the angle on the top the mirror of the bottom. I didn't like that very much so #3 has the same angle on the top and bottom. The diamonds seemed too small, making the pop-up too busy, so I went with 12x6 in #4 and #5. #5 is a refinement of #4 since #4 sticks out of the card when closed. (How many times have I said "Don't do that!"?).

Here you can see the back of the final sketch. All the pieces of paper glued on show where I made a cut or fold, then decided the "remove" it.

![iterating](/images/2602/iterating.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Back of final sketch with band-aids*{: .image-caption}

After I printed the final, full-size design, I needed to know where to score and cut. I marked up the final sketch with row and column numbers, and red and green lines for mountain and valley folds. The two arrows indicate where two edges weren't connected, so tending to float a bit.

![template](/images/2602/template.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Marked up final to help with cutting*{: .image-caption}

It's something that's not too noticeable, but to fix that in the final version I glued a piece of thread on the back where those two edges met.

![thread](/images/2602/thread.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Thread to connect edges*{: .image-caption}

And now there is never a gap.

![align](/images/2602/align.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Floating edges*{: .image-caption}

I couldn't use the {{ site.cameo }} to make the cuts since they needed to be very precise. Cutting the print out by hand was enough to make my eyes cross. In addition to the fold marks on sketch #5, I numbered each column and row so I could transfer the cuts to the final print. (See the `8` below?) To make sure I cut the correct length, I put tape on the ruler to mark where I needed to stop cutting. Here I'm cutting three rows.

![guide](/images/2602/guide.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Tape as cut guide*{: .image-caption}

When making the pattern, I had a choice of making a checkerboard pattern on the base, or adjust the pattern to look like a checkerboard when you looked either straight on or from the top. I tried the latter in sketch #1 and you may notice right above the `1` in the sketch photo there are two black diamonds above each other. Then up higher are two adjacent white squares. When you look at that one at other angles, the mismatched grid is noticeable. So I went with a checkerboard on the base. When you look at the final version straight on, the grid is mismatched as shown below.

![straight](/images/2602/straight-on.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Straight-on view with mismatched grid*{: .image-caption}

But in a more normal viewing angle, the grid matches up nicely.

![front](/images/2602/front.jpg){: width="{{ site.imageWidth }}" }
*Front view with matched grid*{: .image-caption}

I tilted the grid to make it look more interesting that squares, but I keeps seeing them more are squared in perspective than diamonds. Even so, I'm calling this Study in Black and White's shapes diamonds.
